FLAMINGO-P2-010 Transfer Exception Handling — Design Rationale

===================================================================
QUESTION 1: Why are there ten exception classes rather than a simpler grouped model?
===================================================================

The ten classes correspond to ten genuinely distinct conditions that require different handling rules. Grouping them would obscure the differences that matter operationally.

The key distinctions:

EX-001 (precondition invalidity) vs. EX-002 (insufficient allocation): Both are pre-execution failures, but their resolution paths differ. EX-001 may resolve when the investor restores eligibility; EX-002 resolves only if capacity becomes available. Grouping them would require one handling rule to cover both paths.

EX-003 (review stall) is distinct from all other classes: it is not a failure of a technical step but a failure of the review process itself — an admin who hasn't acted. Resolution is operational reassignment, not technical remediation.

EX-004 (chain execution failure) vs. EX-005 (TA instruction rejected): Both are execution failures, but they occur at different layers with different resolution owners. EX-004 can potentially be retried by Flamingo; EX-005 immediately becomes EX-010 because the TA has already received and rejected an instruction.

EX-006 (TA signal timeout) is distinct from EX-005 (TA instruction rejected): EX-006 means nothing has been heard; EX-005 means a negative signal was received. These require different responses — EX-006 begins as inquiry/hold; EX-005 is immediate escalation.

EX-007 (on-chain/registry divergence) vs. EX-008 (wallet revocation mid-transfer): Both involve on-chain state inconsistency, but EX-008 is a specific and immediately actionable condition (wallet was revoked; transfer cannot execute) while EX-007 is a broader divergence that requires investigation before action.

EX-009 (forced correction) and EX-010 (post-TA-instruction escalation): EX-009 is a class; EX-010 is a trigger condition that wraps other classes. EX-010 exists because the presence or absence of a sent TA instruction is the most consequential boundary in exception handling — it determines whether Flamingo can resolve unilaterally or must escalate.

===================================================================
QUESTION 2: Why does EX-010 exist as a separate class rather than as a rule within each other class?
===================================================================

EX-010 is a meta-trigger, not a standalone exception type. But it is defined as a class for two reasons:

1. Auditability: When an EC-012 event is generated for a post-TA-instruction exception, the audit record needs to identify that EX-010 escalation was triggered. If EX-010 were just a rule embedded in other classes, there would be no canonical code to reference in the audit record.

2. Handling consistency: The handling rule for EX-010 is identical regardless of which underlying class triggered it — hold, escalate to Securitize, engage legal counsel if needed. By making it a class, the handling rule is defined once and applied consistently.

An exception that is simultaneously EX-005 and EX-010 is captured as both: the EC-012 event records the underlying class (EX-005 — TA instruction rejected) and the escalation trigger (EX-010 — post-TA-instruction condition). P3-013 implements the escalation routing.

===================================================================
QUESTION 3: Why is EH-002 (no lifecycle shortcuts via exception resolution) treated as absolute?
===================================================================

Because the 8-state canonical chain and the completion boundary rules are locked decisions (LD-022, LD-025–LD-028). Exception handling is an operational control within the platform — it is not a mechanism for the platform to override its own governance rules.

If exception resolution could shortcut the lifecycle, then:
- A forced correction could advance a transfer to LEGALLY_COMPLETE without TA_RECORDED having occurred
- An exception "resolution" could create the appearance of legal completion where none exists
- The completion boundary locks would be effectively unenforceable against a motivated admin with exception resolution authority

EH-002 makes this impossible by defining resolution as "return to a valid state or hold" — never advance past an unsatisfied state. The only way LEGALLY_COMPLETE is reached is by satisfying TA_RECORDED through the normal TA process. No exception resolution path changes that.

===================================================================
QUESTION 4: Why is EH-006 (post-TA-instruction always escalates) an absolute rule rather than context-dependent?
===================================================================

Because Flamingo does not know what the transfer agent has done with an instruction once it is sent.

After TA_INSTRUCTION_SENT, two scenarios are possible:
1. The TA has not yet processed the instruction
2. The TA has processed the instruction (possibly partially or fully)

Flamingo has no guaranteed way to determine which scenario is true at the time an exception is identified. If Flamingo were to unilaterally "cancel" a transfer in its Operational Registry while the TA is simultaneously processing the instruction, the result would be:
- Flamingo's registry showing the transfer cancelled
- The TA completing the legal recordation
- The investor's legal holder-of-record status advanced while Flamingo's records show cancelled

This is a divergence that has legal consequences for the investor and creates a serious compliance problem.

The conservative rule — any post-TA-instruction exception escalates to Securitize — prevents this. Until Securitize confirms what reversal/cancellation mechanisms exist (UI-P2-010-004), the correct posture is: we cannot resolve this unilaterally.

===================================================================
QUESTION 5: Why is EX-007 (on-chain/registry divergence) not automatically resolved by updating the registry to match on-chain state?
===================================================================

Because the divergence may indicate a problem in either direction. Two scenarios:

Scenario A: The Operational Registry missed an on-chain event (e.g., a chain-executed event was not logged). In this case, updating the registry to match on-chain state is correct — and should be documented as a correction.

Scenario B: An on-chain event occurred that the Flamingo platform did not authorize (e.g., a token transfer executed on-chain that does not correspond to an APPROVED transfer in the workflow). In this case, updating the registry to match on-chain state would silently accept an unauthorized transaction.

If the handling rule were "update registry to match on-chain," Scenario B would never be caught — the unauthorized transaction would be retroactively accepted.

The correct rule is: detect the divergence, investigate which scenario it is, then take the appropriate action. LD-041 (Base-wins) means on-chain state is authoritative for restriction enforcement — but it does not mean all on-chain events should be retroactively accepted into the registry without investigation.

===================================================================
QUESTION 6: Why are transfer exceptions and reconciliation breaks (P2-014) defined as distinct?
===================================================================

They have different scopes and different detection mechanisms:

Transfer exceptions are detected per-transfer during processing. An admin reviewing a transfer, or a system event during processing, identifies that something is wrong with this specific transfer. The detection is synchronous with the transfer workflow.

Reconciliation breaks are identified during a reconciliation run — a comparison of Flamingo's Operational Registry against TA state or on-chain state across a population of records. Detection is periodic and aggregate.

A single EX-006 timeout may or may not be visible in a reconciliation run, depending on timing. A reconciliation break may surface a pattern of EX-007 divergences that were individually noticed but not fully resolved.

Defining them separately allows:
- Transfer exceptions to be handled per-transfer by the transfer administrator
- Reconciliation breaks to be handled as a separate reconciliation/operations function
- The escalation hand-off between the two to be explicit (§13)

Without the separation, an EX-006 exception that was escalated and remains open could be double-counted or re-processed during a reconciliation run, creating confusion about which workflow is responsible for resolution.
